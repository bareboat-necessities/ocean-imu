    \begin{abstract}
        \texttt{SeaMetrics} is a real-time estimator of ocean wave spectral metrics
        derived from a single vertical acceleration signal $a_z(t)$ and an
        externally tracked instantaneous frequency $\omega_\text{inst}(t)$.
        The system demodulates the acceleration record into a displacement envelope
        power time series, accumulates running spectral moments, and synthesises a
        comprehensive suite of oceanographic quantities on every update cycle.
        Outputs range from zeroth-order wave energy and significant wave height
        through spectral bandwidth, groupiness, nonlinearity, shallow-water
        dispersion corrections, breaking probability, and human comfort indices,
        to extreme-value return levels estimated via Weibull and
        peak-over-threshold analysis.
        A first-order bias-correction scheme compensates for the Jensen-inequality
        inflation that afflicts inverse-frequency moments under noisy tracking.
        The complete pipeline operates sample-by-sample with constant memory and
        is suited to deployment on embedded and shipborne measurement systems.
    \end{abstract}

    \begin{IEEEkeywords}
        ocean wave spectra, spectral moments, significant wave height,
        wave period, online estimation, embedded signal processing,
        bandwidth, groupiness, breaking probability, motion sickness
    \end{IEEEkeywords}

    \section{Introduction}

    Characterising the sea state from an onboard sensor poses a classical
    estimation problem: the underlying directional wave spectrum is
    inaccessible, yet reliable proxies for its integral properties can be
    extracted from a single well-calibrated scalar record.
    \texttt{SeaMetrics} exploits the fact that a vertically accelerating
    platform experiences a signal whose power spectral density is proportional
    to $\omega^4$ times the displacement spectrum, allowing spectral moments
    to be accumulated from the demodulated displacement envelope.

    The tool accepts two inputs: the vertical acceleration $a_z(t)$ measured at
    the sensor location, and an instantaneous frequency estimate
    $\omega_\text{inst}(t)$ supplied by an external tracker such as the
    Aranovskiy adaptive observer \cite{Bobtsov2013}.
    From these, a running estimate of the displacement envelope power
    $P_\eta(t)$ is maintained, and spectral moments of integer order
    $n \in \{-1, 0, 1, 2, 3, 4\}$ are accumulated as exponentially weighted
    averages.
    The complete set of derived quantities—periods, heights, bandwidths,
    nonlinear corrections, comfort indices, and extreme-value statistics—is
    recomputed on every sample, enabling true streaming oceanographic analysis
    without buffering or batch FFT processing.

    Section~\ref{sec:moments} defines the spectral moments and their physical
    interpretation.
    Sections~\ref{sec:central}--\ref{sec:groupiness} derive the standard
    oceanographic parameters that flow from these moments.
    Section~\ref{sec:extremes} covers extreme-value analysis.
    Sections~\ref{sec:shallow}--\ref{sec:quality} address shallow-water
    corrections, environmental coupling, and data quality diagnostics.
    Section~\ref{sec:bias} gives the first-order bias-correction theory.
    Section~\ref{sec:summary} provides a consolidated reference table of
    all output quantities.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
    \section{Spectral Moments}
    \label{sec:moments}

    Let $P_\eta(t) \ge 0$ be the instantaneous displacement envelope power
    and $\omega(t) = \omega_\text{inst}(t) > 0$ the instantaneous angular
    frequency.
    The $n$-th spectral moment is defined as the running expectation
    \begin{equation}
        M_n \;=\; \bigl\langle P_\eta(t)\;\omega(t)^n \bigr\rangle,
        \qquad n \in \{-1,\,0,\,1,\,2,\,3,\,4\}.
        \label{eq:moments}
    \end{equation}
    In the ensemble sense, \eqref{eq:moments} coincides with the frequency-domain
    integral $\int \omega^n S(\omega)\,\mathrm{d}\omega$ over the one-sided
    spectrum $S(\omega)$ whenever the narrowband assumption holds \cite{Ochi1998}.

    The physical roles of each moment are as follows:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $M_0$: total variance of surface displacement, proportional to
        the potential energy per unit area of the wave field; the
        conventional measure of ``wave energy.''
        \item $M_1$: energy-weighted frequency; its ratio with $M_0$ gives the
        mean angular frequency of the sea state.
        \item $M_2$: energy-weighted mean-square frequency; directly linked to
        the wave slope variance and the zero-upcrossing rate.
        \item $M_3$, $M_4$: higher-order moments controlling the spectral
        skewness and kurtosis, and enabling peakedness diagnostics.
        \item $M_{-1}$: the negative-order moment, which enters the definition
        of the energy flux period and the mean wave energy period $T_{-1,0}$.
    \end{itemize}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
    \section{Central Moments and Spectral Shape}
    \label{sec:central}

    The central spectral moments characterise the shape of the wave spectrum
    relative to its mean frequency $\bar\omega = M_1/M_0$.
    They are related to the raw moments by
    \begin{align}
        \mu_2 &= \frac{M_2}{M_0} - \bar\omega^2,
        \label{eq:mu2} \\
        \mu_3 &= \frac{M_3}{M_0} - 3\bar\omega\frac{M_2}{M_0} + 2\bar\omega^3,
        \label{eq:mu3} \\
        \mu_4 &= \frac{M_4}{M_0} - 4\bar\omega\frac{M_3}{M_0}
        + 6\bar\omega^2\frac{M_2}{M_0} - 3\bar\omega^4.
        \label{eq:mu4}
    \end{align}

    The second central moment $\mu_2$ measures spectral spread:
    a narrow swell peak has small $\mu_2$, while a fully developed sea
    has large $\mu_2$.
    The third central moment $\mu_3$ quantifies asymmetry: positive values
    indicate a high-frequency tail heavier than the low-frequency shoulder,
    which arises during wind-sea generation.
    The fourth central moment $\mu_4$ measures spectral peakedness relative
    to a Gaussian reference shape.

    Three normalised shape diagnostics follow immediately:
    \begin{equation}
        \text{Skewness} = \frac{\mu_3}{\mu_2^{3/2}},
        \quad
        \text{Kurtosis} = \frac{\mu_4}{\mu_2^2},
        \quad
        \text{Excess kurtosis} = \frac{\mu_4}{\mu_2^2} - 3.
    \end{equation}

    \noindent
    Three peakedness parameters based on the same moment ratios appear in the
    engineering literature:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Ochi peakedness \cite{Ochi1998}:
        $Q_p = M_0 M_4/M_2^2$.
        \item Benassai parameter: identical definition to $Q_p$, widely used
        in coastal structure design.
        \item Goda peakedness \cite{Goda1970}:
        $Q_G = 2(Q_p - 1)$.
    \end{itemize}
    All three are dimensionless and increase as the spectrum narrows toward
    a monochromatic sea.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
    \section{Frequency Metrics}
    \label{sec:freq}

    The mean angular frequency and its equivalent in hertz are
    \begin{equation}
        \bar\omega = \frac{M_1}{M_0}, \qquad \bar{f} = \frac{\bar\omega}{2\pi}.
    \end{equation}

    The relative bandwidth (RBW) is the canonical dimensionless measure of
    spectral width:
    \begin{equation}
        \text{RBW} = \frac{\sqrt{\mu_2}}{\bar\omega}.
        \label{eq:rbw}
    \end{equation}
    $\text{RBW} \ll 1$ characterises a narrow swell; $\text{RBW} \sim 0.5$
    is typical of a mixed wind-sea and swell condition.

    An independent bandwidth estimate is available from the \emph{phase-increment
    RBW}, computed as the normalised variance of successive phase increments
    $\Delta\phi/\Delta t$.
    This estimator is insensitive to amplitude fluctuations and therefore
    provides a useful consistency check against the moment-based RBW,
    particularly when envelope demodulation is imperfect.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
    \section{Regularity Metrics}
    \label{sec:regularity}

    Regularity quantifies how closely the wave record resembles a
    deterministic sinusoid.

    The \emph{spectral regularity} decays exponentially with bandwidth:
    \begin{equation}
        R_\text{spec} = \exp(-\beta\cdot\text{RBW}),
    \end{equation}
    where $\beta$ is a tunable decay constant.
    A value of $R_\text{spec} \approx 1$ indicates a near-monochromatic sea;
    $R_\text{spec} \approx 0$ indicates a broad, irregular spectrum.

    The \emph{phase regularity} is sensitive to phase coherence rather than
    amplitude spread:
    \begin{equation}
        R_\text{phase} = \left\lVert\left\langle \frac{z}{|z|} \right\rangle\right\rVert,
    \end{equation}
    where $z$ is the complex analytic signal formed from the demodulated envelope.

    The classical \emph{spectral narrowness parameter} \cite{Cartwright1956} is
    \begin{equation}
        \nu = \sqrt{\frac{M_0 M_2}{M_1^2} - 1},
    \end{equation}
    which vanishes for a monochromatic wave and grows with spectral breadth.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
    \section{Wave Period Estimates}
    \label{sec:periods}

    Several period definitions arise naturally from different moment ratios.
    Each carries a distinct physical meaning and is standard in different
    branches of ocean engineering:
    \begin{align}
        T_z &= \sqrt{2\pi^2\,\frac{M_0}{M_2}}
        && \text{(mean zero-crossing period)}, \label{eq:Tz}\\
        T_1 &= \frac{2\pi M_0}{M_1}
        && \text{(energy-weighted mean period)}, \label{eq:T1}\\
        T_{m02} &= 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{M_0}{M_2}}
        && \text{(variance-based mean period)}, \label{eq:Tm02}\\
        T_e &= \frac{2\pi M_{-1}}{M_0}
        && \text{(energy flux period)}, \label{eq:Te}\\
        T_{m0,-1} &= \frac{M_0}{M_{-1}}, \quad
        T_{m1,-1} = \frac{M_1}{M_{-1}}.
    \end{align}

    The mean zero-upcrossing rate—the expected number of complete wave cycles
    per second—follows from Rice's formula \cite{Longuet1963}:
    \begin{equation}
        \nu_\uparrow = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{M_2}{M_0}}.
        \label{eq:upcrossing}
    \end{equation}
    The reciprocal $1/\nu_\uparrow$ coincides with $T_{m02}$ up to a numerical
    factor and provides a direct estimate of average wave count over any
    observation window.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
    \section{Wave Heights and Steepness}
    \label{sec:heights}

    Under the Rayleigh amplitude assumption, the surface displacement
    has standard deviation
    \begin{equation}
        \sigma = \sqrt{M_0}
    \end{equation}
    and the significant wave height is
    \begin{equation}
        H_s = 2\sqrt{2}\,\sigma \approx 4\sigma.
        \label{eq:Hs}
    \end{equation}
    The factor $2\sqrt{2}$ corresponds to the mean of the highest one-third
    of waves in a Rayleigh distribution \cite{Ochi1998}.

    Deep-water wavelength is approximated from $T_z$ via the linear dispersion
    relation $\omega^2 = gk$:
    \begin{equation}
        L_0 = \frac{g T_z^2}{2\pi}.
    \end{equation}
    Wave steepness, the primary nonlinearity indicator in deep water, is then
    \begin{equation}
        \text{St} = \frac{H_s}{L_0}.
    \end{equation}
    Steep waves ($\text{St} > 0.05$) are subject to whitecapping and
    modulational instability; limiting steepness in deep water is
    $\text{St}_\text{max} \approx 0.142$ (Miche criterion).

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
    \section{Crest Probabilities and Extremes}
    \label{sec:probabilities}

    \subsection{Rayleigh Exceedance}

    For a narrow-band Gaussian sea the crest height $H_c$ follows the
    Rayleigh distribution \cite{Cartwright1956}:
    \begin{equation}
        P(H_c > h) = \exp\!\left(-\frac{h^2}{2\sigma^2}\right).
        \label{eq:rayleigh}
    \end{equation}

    \subsection{Nonlinear Tayfun Correction}

    For steep, near-breaking seas the Rayleigh model underestimates
    large-crest probabilities.
    Tayfun's second-order correction \cite{Tayfun1980} gives
    \begin{equation}
        P(H_c > h) \approx
        \exp\!\left(-\frac{h^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)
        \exp\!\left(\Lambda\frac{h^3}{\sigma^3}\right),
    \end{equation}
    where $\Lambda$ is proportional to the wave steepness $\text{St}$.
    This model is recommended for wave-structure interaction design in
    steep-sea conditions.

    \subsection{Characteristic Crest Heights}

    High-percentile crests are estimated by inverting \eqref{eq:rayleigh}:
    \begin{equation}
        H_{1/N} = \sigma\sqrt{2\ln N}, \qquad N \in \{10,\,100\}.
    \end{equation}

    The expected maximum crest in a record containing $N$ waves is
    approximated by the Gumbel asymptote:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathbb{E}[H_\text{max}] \approx
        \sigma\!\left(\sqrt{2\ln N} + \frac{\gamma_\text{E}}{\sqrt{2\ln N}}\right),
    \end{equation}
    where $\gamma_\text{E} \approx 0.5772$ is the Euler–Mascheroni constant
    and $N = \nu_\uparrow\, T$ for a record of duration $T$.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
    \section{Spectral Bandwidth Parameters}
    \label{sec:bandwidth}

    Four complementary definitions of spectral bandwidth appear in the
    standard literature and are all computed by \texttt{SeaMetrics}:
    \begin{align}
        \varepsilon_\text{CLH} &= \sqrt{1 - \frac{M_1^2}{M_0 M_2}}
        && \text{\cite{Cartwright1956}},
        \label{eq:eCLH}\\
        \varepsilon_\text{Goda} &= \sqrt{\frac{M_0 M_2}{M_1^2} - 1}
        && \text{\cite{Goda1970}},
        \label{eq:eGoda}\\
        \varepsilon_\text{Kuik} &= \frac{\sqrt{M_0 M_2 - M_1^2}}{M_1}
        && \text{\cite{Kuik1988}},
        \label{eq:eKuik}\\
        \varepsilon_\text{LH} &= \sqrt{\frac{M_0 M_2}{M_1^2} - 1}
        && \text{(Longuet--Higgins)}.
        \label{eq:eLH}
    \end{align}
    All four vanish for a monochromatic wave; their numerical differences
    reflect the choice of normalization and are small for narrow spectra.
    $\varepsilon_\text{CLH}$ is bounded in $[0,1]$ and is particularly
    convenient for design charts.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
    \section{Groupiness and Modulational Instability}
    \label{sec:groupiness}

    \subsection{Groupiness Factor}

    Natural wave fields travel in groups whose envelope modulates slowly
    compared to the carrier frequency.
    The groupiness factor
    \begin{equation}
        G = \frac{T_g}{T_z}
    \end{equation}
    measures the ratio of the average group period $T_g$ to the mean
    zero-crossing period $T_z$.
    Large values of $G$ indicate pronounced grouping and are associated with
    enhanced structural loads on offshore platforms.

    \subsection{Benjamin--Feir Index}

    The Benjamin--Feir Index (BFI) quantifies the tendency of a wave train
    to develop modulational (sideband) instability through the
    Benjamin--Feir mechanism \cite{BenjaminFeir1967}:
    \begin{equation}
        \text{BFI} = \frac{\sqrt{2}\;H_s/L_0}{\Delta f / f_p},
    \end{equation}
    where $f_p$ is the peak frequency and $\Delta f$ is the spectral bandwidth.
    $\text{BFI} > 1$ signals conditions prone to freak-wave formation and is a
    key input to rogue-wave risk assessment.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
    \section{Wave Energy and Power}
    \label{sec:energy}

    The mean wave energy density per unit surface area in deep water is
    \begin{equation}
        E = \rho g\,M_0,
    \end{equation}
    where $\rho$ is seawater density.
    The energy flux period relates the negative-order moment to the wave
    energy budget:
    \begin{equation}
        T_{e,\text{flux}} = \frac{M_{-1}}{M_0}.
    \end{equation}

    Deep-water wave power per unit crest length is given by the standard
    linear formula:
    \begin{equation}
        P = \frac{\rho g^2}{64\pi}\,H_s^2\,T_e,
        \label{eq:power}
    \end{equation}
    which is proportional to the square of the significant wave height and
    linearly proportional to the energy period.
    Equation~\eqref{eq:power} is the standard metric used to assess
    wave-energy converter (WEC) resource.

    \section{Nonlinear and Development Metrics}
    \label{sec:nonlinear}

    Three parameters characterise the degree of nonlinearity or development
    of the sea state:

    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Ursell number:}
        $Ur = H L^2 / h^3$
        expresses the competition between nonlinearity and frequency
        dispersion in water of finite depth $h$.
        Deep-water waves have $Ur \ll 1$; shallow-water cnoidal waves
        have $Ur \gg 1$.
        \item \textbf{Nonlinearity parameter:}
        $n = \tfrac{1}{2}H_s k$,
        a dimensionless spectral-peak steepness that scales directly with
        the amplitude of Stokes-type bound harmonics.
        \item \textbf{Wave age:}
        $c_p/U_{10}$,
        the ratio of the spectral peak phase speed to the 10-metre wind
        speed.  Values less than unity indicate a young, actively forced
        wind sea; values exceeding unity indicate mature or swell-dominated
        conditions.
    \end{itemize}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
    \section{Wave Breaking}
    \label{sec:breaking}

    Two proxy indicators of wave breaking probability are maintained:

    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Depth-limited breaking:}
        $P_b^\text{depth} = \exp\!\bigl(-0.5(0.78\,h/H_s)^2\bigr)$,
        which rises sharply as the wave height approaches the
        depth-limited threshold $H_b \approx 0.78\,h$.
        \item \textbf{Steepness-limited breaking:}
        $P_b^\text{steep} = 10\,(H_s/L_0)$,
        a linear proxy that captures whitecapping in deep water as
        steepness increases toward the Miche limit.
    \end{itemize}

    The depth-limited maximum crest height is $H_b \approx 0.78\,h$
    (Battjes--Janssen \cite{Battjes1978}).
    Both indicators are available simultaneously to support integrated
    near-shore and offshore analyses.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
    \section{Human Comfort and Motion Sickness}
    \label{sec:comfort}

    \subsection{Motion Sickness Dose Value}

    The ISO~2631 standard \cite{ISO2631} defines the Motion Sickness Dose
    Value (MSDV) as the frequency-weighted root-mean-square acceleration
    integrated over the exposure duration $T$:
    \begin{equation}
        \text{MSDV} = \sqrt{M_2}\,\sqrt{T}.
    \end{equation}
    The factor $\sqrt{M_2}$ is the RMS of vertical velocity increment,
    which captures the $\sim\SI{0.2}{\hertz}$ sensitivity peak of the
    human vestibular system.

    \subsection{Derived Comfort Metrics}

    From MSDV, the following application quantities are derived:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Seasickness incidence:} percentage of an average
        population expected to vomit after exposure, computed from
        an ISO-calibrated logistic model of MSDV, frequency, and
        population susceptibility.
        \item \textbf{Comfort rating:} a heuristic 0--100 scale in which
        100 represents a flat calm and 0 represents severe discomfort;
        mapped monotonically from MSDV via an empirical transfer function.
        \item \textbf{Vertical motion intensity:} $\sqrt{M_2}$, expressed as
        an RMS vertical acceleration useful for crew workload assessment.
        \item \textbf{Time to onset of discomfort:}
        an empirical estimate proportional to $(\text{accel} \cdot f^{0.7})^{-1}$,
        giving the approximate latency before the average occupant
        experiences noticeable discomfort.
    \end{itemize}

    \section{Extreme Value Analysis}
    \label{sec:extremes}

    \subsection{Weibull Return Heights}

    For long-return-period design, block maxima of the crest height
    distribution are fitted with a two-parameter Weibull model.
    Given a shape parameter $\alpha$ and scale parameter $\beta$,
    the return height for return period $T_\text{ret}$ satisfies
    \begin{equation}
        H_{T_\text{ret}} = \beta
        \!\left(\ln\frac{T_\text{ret}}{T_\text{obs}}\right)^{1/\alpha},
    \end{equation}
    where $T_\text{obs}$ is the observation window duration.
    This approach accounts for non-Rayleigh tail behaviour observed in
    steep or shallow-water spectra.

    \subsection{Peak-Over-Threshold Analysis}

    The mean excess function above a threshold $u$ provides an alternative
    extreme-value diagnostic:
    \begin{equation}
        e(u) = \mathbb{E}[H_c - u \mid H_c > u].
    \end{equation}
    A linearly increasing $e(u)$ as a function of $u$ is consistent with a
    generalised Pareto tail; the slope gives the shape parameter of the
    exceedance distribution.
    This metric is particularly informative for storm-peak identification
    and mooring fatigue analysis.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
    \section{Shallow-Water Extensions}
    \label{sec:shallow}

    When the sensor is deployed in finite water depth $h$, the deep-water
    dispersion relation $\omega^2 = gk$ is replaced by
    \begin{equation}
        \omega^2 = gk\tanh(kh),
        \label{eq:dispersion}
    \end{equation}
    which must be solved iteratively for the wavenumber $k$ given
    $\omega$ and $h$.
    Three additional shallow-water metrics are computed:

    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Shoaling coefficient $K_s$:} accounts for the change in
        wave amplitude caused by variation of the group velocity
        $c_g = \mathrm{d}\omega/\mathrm{d}k$ with depth; it is used to
        back-calculate offshore wave conditions from near-shore
        observations.
        \item \textbf{Refraction coefficient:} derived from Snell's law
        $c = \omega/k$, it describes the change in wave direction as the
        bathymetric gradient rotates wave crests toward the depth contours.
        \item \textbf{Battjes--Janssen breaking probability \cite{Battjes1978}:}
        a depth-parameterised model that represents wave dissipation by
        random breaking as a fraction of waves exceeding the depth-limited
        height $\gamma h$, where $\gamma \approx 0.78$ is the breaker index.
    \end{itemize}

    \section{Environmental Coupling and Application Metrics}
    \label{sec:env}

    \subsection{Wave Age and Sea--Swell Partition}

    When a wind speed $U_{10}$ is available, the wave age $c_p/U_{10}$
    enables categorical classification of the sea state as young
    ($c_p/U_{10} < 0.8$), mature ($0.8$--$1.2$), or swell-dominated
    ($c_p/U_{10} > 1.2$).
    The fraction of total spectral energy attributable to swell, rather than
    locally generated wind sea, is estimated from this classification and
    provides guidance for ship motion prediction and route optimisation.

    \subsection{Radiation Stresses}

    Scalar proxies for the depth-integrated wave momentum flux components
    \begin{equation}
        S_{xx} \propto H_s^2,\qquad S_{yy} \propto H_s^2
    \end{equation}
    are maintained as diagnostics relevant to wave-driven coastal setup,
    longshore current generation, and sediment transport modelling.

    \subsection{Bottom Orbital Velocity}

    The near-bed peak orbital velocity is estimated from linear wave theory
    at depth $h$:
    \begin{equation}
        u_b = \frac{\pi H_s}{T_z\,\sinh(kh)},
    \end{equation}
    which governs sediment mobilisation and scour around submerged
    structures.

    \subsection{WEC Capture Width Ratio}

    For wave energy harvesting applications, the capture width ratio
    \begin{equation}
        \text{CWR} = \frac{P_\text{absorbed}}{P\,D}
    \end{equation}
    is the ratio of power absorbed by a device of aperture $D$ to the
    incident deep-water wave power $P$ from \eqref{eq:power} per unit crest
    length.
    A theoretical maximum of $\text{CWR} = 1/(2\pi)$ applies to a point
    absorber in deep water.

    \section{Data Quality and Diagnostics}
    \label{sec:quality}

    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Temporal stability:} the running variance of $H_s$
        evaluated over a sliding reference window provides a
        non-stationarity indicator; large values signal a rapidly
        evolving sea state or sensor drift that may invalidate
        stationarity assumptions underlying moment-based estimators.
        \item \textbf{Band-limited SNR:} the ratio of in-band variance to total
        variance for a user-specified frequency band quantifies how much
        spectral energy falls outside the wave band of interest, and is
        useful for detecting sensor noise floors or spurious mechanical
        resonances.
        \item \textbf{Data gap fraction:} the fraction of samples rejected as
        non-finite is tracked continuously; values above a few percent
        indicate an intermittent sensor or communication fault that should
        be investigated before trusting the spectral estimates.
    \end{itemize}

    \section{Bias Correction}
    \label{sec:bias}

    \subsection{Motivation}

    When the instantaneous frequency estimate $\omega(t)$ carries
    measurement noise with variance $\sigma_\omega^2$, any moment of the
    form $M_n$ with $n < 0$ is systematically biased upward by Jensen's
    inequality, since the function $\omega \mapsto \omega^{-k}$ is convex
    for $k > 0$:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathbb{E}\!\left[\frac{1}{\omega^k}\right] >
        \frac{1}{\mathbb{E}[\omega]^k}.
    \end{equation}
    The same inflation propagates into all quantities derived from $M_{-1}$
    and into any ratio where the denominator involves a high positive power
    of $\omega$.
    Under jittery tracking—typical of low-frequency swell or turbulent
    sea conditions—the uncorrected $H_s$ and $T_z$ may be overestimated
    by several percent.

    \subsection{First-Order Taylor Correction}

    Expanding $\omega^{-n}$ around $\bar\omega = \mathbb{E}[\omega]$
    to second order gives
    \begin{equation}
        \mathbb{E}\!\left[\omega^{-n}\right] \approx
        \frac{1}{\bar\omega^n}
        \left(1 + c_n\,\frac{\sigma_\omega^2}{\bar\omega^2}\right),
        \label{eq:bias_expansion}
    \end{equation}
    with polynomial coefficients $c_n = n(n+1)/2$, yielding
    \begin{equation}
        c_{-1} = 1,\quad c_{-2} = 3,\quad c_{-3} = 6,\quad c_{-4} = 10.
    \end{equation}
    Inverting \eqref{eq:bias_expansion} gives bias-corrected spectral moments:
    \begin{align}
        M_0^c &= \frac{M_0}{1 + 10\,\sigma_\omega^2/\bar\omega^2},
        \label{eq:M0c}\\
        M_1^c &= \frac{M_1}{1 + 6\,\sigma_\omega^2/\bar\omega^2},
        \label{eq:M1c}\\
        M_2^c &= \frac{M_2}{1 + 3\,\sigma_\omega^2/\bar\omega^2},
        \label{eq:M2c}\\
        M_3^c &= \frac{M_3}{1 + \sigma_\omega^2/\bar\omega^2},
        \label{eq:M3c}\\
        M_4^c &= M_4 \qquad\text{(no correction required)},
        \label{eq:M4c}\\
        M_{-1}^c &= \frac{M_{-1}}{1 + 6\,\sigma_\omega^2/\bar\omega^2}.
        \label{eq:Mm1c}
    \end{align}
    All higher-level derived quantities—periods, heights, bandwidths,
    groupiness, energy flux, and comfort indices—are computed consistently
    from the corrected set $\{M_n^c\}$ when correction is enabled.

    \subsection{Practical Effect}

    Bias correction most noticeably reduces spurious inflation in the
    significant wave height $H_s$, zero-crossing period $T_z$, and
    relative bandwidth under jittery frequency tracking or residual
    DC offset in the acceleration signal.
    It is especially beneficial at low frequencies (long-period swell)
    where the Aranovskiy observer's adaptation rate may induce
    temporary frequency jitter during sea-state transitions.
    The correction is always conservative in the sense that it reduces
    rather than inflates the estimated quantities, providing a closer
    lower bound on the true spectral moments.

    \section{Conclusion}

    \texttt{SeaMetrics} provides a rigorous and comprehensive real-time
    pipeline for ocean wave characterisation from a single vertical
    acceleration channel.
    The moment-based architecture ensures that all derived quantities are
    internally consistent, continuously updated, and computable with
    constant computational cost per sample.
    The bias-correction layer makes the system robust to jittery frequency
    tracking, which is the dominant source of systematic error in
    embedded adaptive-observer implementations.
    The full output set—spanning standard oceanographic parameters,
    nonlinear crest statistics, shallow-water corrections, human comfort
    indices, and extreme-value return levels—supports applications ranging
    from recreational sailing safety to offshore structure design and
    wave energy resource assessment.
